Every photographer learns to chase the hour after sunrise and before sunset. The light
goes warm, soft, and directional, and everything looks better. That's not folklore — it's
a direct consequence of how air scatters light.

## Blue scatters more than red

Air molecules scatter short wavelengths far more strongly than long ones. The strength of
this **Rayleigh scattering** falls off with the fourth power of wavelength:

$$
I_\text{scattered} \propto \frac{1}{\lambda^4}
$$

Blue light ($\lambda \approx 450\,\text{nm}$) scatters roughly
$\left(\frac{700}{450}\right)^4 \approx 5.9\times$ more than red light
($\lambda \approx 700\,\text{nm}$). That's why the midday sky is blue: you're seeing
sunlight that bounced sideways into your eyes, and it's mostly the blue that bounced.

## The sun's light takes a longer road at dawn

At noon, sunlight punches almost straight down through the atmosphere. At sunrise and
sunset, it skims in at a shallow angle, traveling through far more air to reach you. The
**air mass** — the relative path length — rises sharply as the sun approaches the horizon:

$$
\text{AM} \approx \frac{1}{\cos\theta_z}
$$

where $\theta_z$ is the sun's angle from straight overhead. At noon ($\theta_z = 0$),
$\text{AM} = 1$. When the sun is $80^\circ$ from vertical — low and golden —
$\text{AM} \approx 5.8$. Nearly six atmospheres of air to cross.

> A longer path means more blue gets scattered *out* of the beam before it reaches you.
> What's left over is weighted toward red and orange. The sun itself looks warm because
> the blue has been stripped away en route.

## Why it also looks soft

Two more things happen at once:

- **The light turns sideways into fill.** Because so much short-wavelength light is
  scattering around the sky, shadows are filled by a broad, diffuse glow rather than a
  single harsh source. Contrast drops.
- **The angle rakes across your subject.** Low light grazes surfaces, revealing texture
  and casting long, gentle shadows that model form instead of flattening it.

So "golden hour" is really three physics facts wearing a trench coat: $1/\lambda^4$
scattering, a $1/\cos\theta_z$ path length, and a low angle of incidence. Warm colour,
soft fill, raking shadow — all from the sun simply being low in the sky.

---

The practical takeaway needs no equations: shoot when the sun is low, put it behind or
beside your subject, and let the atmosphere do the colour grading for free.
