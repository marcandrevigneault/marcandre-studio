When you grow without soil, you take on a job the soil used to do for free: deciding,
precisely, what the roots get to drink. That sounds intimidating. It isn't — it mostly
comes down to one number and a little arithmetic.

## The one number: electrical conductivity

Pure water barely conducts electricity. Dissolve mineral salts in it — nitrates,
phosphates, potassium — and it conducts much better. So we can *measure* how much
nutrient is in solution simply by measuring how well the water conducts. That's
**electrical conductivity (EC)**, usually reported in millisiemens per centimetre
(mS/cm).

A useful rule of thumb relates EC to total dissolved solids:

$$
\text{ppm} \approx k \cdot \text{EC} \quad\text{where}\quad k \in [500, 700]
$$

The conversion factor $k$ depends on which standard your meter uses — a "500 scale"
meter and a "700 scale" meter will read the *same* water differently. This is the single
most common source of confusion for beginners, so always note the scale your meter uses.

> A typical leafy-green reservoir runs around **EC 1.4–1.8 mS/cm**. Fruiting crops like
> tomatoes want more — often **EC 2.0–3.0**.

## Why the ratio matters more than the total

Plants don't drink nutrients in arbitrary proportions. The classic Hoagland-style ratio
keeps nitrogen, potassium, and the rest in balance. If you only chase a target EC, you
can hit the right *amount* of salt while having the wrong *kind* — too much of one ion,
not enough of another.

Here's the mass of a single nutrient you need to reach a target concentration $C$
(in mg/L) in a reservoir of volume $V$ (in litres):

$$
m = \frac{C \cdot V}{1000} \quad [\text{grams}]
$$

So to hit 150 mg/L of nitrogen in a 40 L reservoir:

```python
C = 150   # mg/L target
V = 40    # litres
mass_g = C * V / 1000
print(mass_g)   # -> 6.0 grams of N (as elemental nitrogen)
```

In practice you don't dose elemental nitrogen — you dose a *fertiliser salt* that's only
partly nitrogen, so you divide by that fraction. Calcium nitrate, for instance, is about
15.5% nitrogen by mass, so you'd need roughly `6.0 / 0.155 ≈ 38.7 g` of it.

## A sane mixing routine

1. **Start with the right water.** Reverse-osmosis or rainwater gives you a known
   baseline (EC ≈ 0). Tap water already carries dissolved minerals — measure it first.
2. **Add nutrients, then mix, then measure.** Never trust the label's "X mL per litre"
   blindly; meters don't lie, labels round.
3. **Adjust pH last.** Most crops absorb nutrients best between **pH 5.5 and 6.2**. Adjust
   *after* the salts are in, because adding them shifts pH on its own.
4. **Top up with plain water, not nutrients.** As plants drink, water leaves faster than
   salt — so EC *rises* over time. Counterintuitive, but it's why you top up with water.

---

That's the whole mental model: conductivity tells you *how much*, the ratio tells you
*what kind*, and pH decides whether the roots can actually take it up. Get those three
talking to each other and the rest is observation.
